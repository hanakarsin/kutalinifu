Analysis: Mukhisa Kituyi's hurdles in quest to be Kenya's 5th president

Posted by Enda Jantzen on Saturday, May 25, 2024

- Mukhisa Kituyi is facing opposition from his Western Kenya backyard which has four presidential candidates so far

- The former UNCTAD secretary general has been out of the country for more than seven years and his personal touch with the people has shrunk significantly

- Mukhisa is also seen as an elitist who hardly interacts with people in the villages and will be fighting to change the narrative

- A section of Luhya elders have sneered at his ambition and thrown their weight behind Musalia Mudavadi

The declaration by the former United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) secretary general Mukhisa Kituyi that he will be on the presidential ballot in 2022 has received mixed reactions from Kenyans.

While dozens have expressed confidence in his ability to turn around the country's ailing economy considering his vast experience and unmatched profile in the field, doubts have equally been cast upon his ability to marshal sufficient support to propel him to power in 2022.

TUKO.co.ke looks at some of the hurdles the former UNACTAD will have to grapple with in his quest to become Kenya's fifth president.

Wetang'ula, Mudavadi unity hard to break

One of the reasons why Mukhisa's presidential bid has not been taken with the seriousness it deserves is because of the long-standing inter-clan divisions that have been defining the uneven voting patterns of the Luhya people.

Before he joined the race to State House, Western region already had three presidential; Musalia Mudavadi (ANC), Moses Wetang'ula (FORD Kenya) and Kakamega Governor Wycliffe Oparanya.

Wetangu'la who is also the Bungoma senator has joined forces with Mudavadi and chances are they may easily agree on who should represent the region in the contest.

The two leaders hail from the two most populous subtribes out of the 17 sub-tribes that form the Luhya Nation.

According to 2019 census, Bukusu is the largest subtribe with over 1 million people followed by Maragoli estimated to be about 400, 000.

In local terms, Wetangula is the de facto political leader of the Bukusu people while Mudavadi holds the same title among the Maragoli.

Mukhisa hails from Bungoma county and belongs to the Bukusu subtribe.

Political analysts argue it would be difficult for Mukhisa to get sufficient votes from the Western region without the support of Wetang'ula and Mudavadi

"It will not be easy for him (Mukhisa) to penetrate and get significant votes from Western considering the strides Wetangu'la and Mudavadi have made while he was away. It is harder now that two are working together to consolidate the Luhya vote," said political analyst Nyukuri Barasa.

Javan Karani, a communication strategist, noted Mukhisa can only move masses in Western region if gets the endorsement of ODM leader Raila Odinga or President Uhuru Kenya or both.

"The reason why Mudavadi and Wetang'ula are working together is because they want to lock Raila out of Western. But remember this, although people are tired of Raila, they have not given up on him, so if he comes with someone like Mukhisa together with Oparanya then he can cause trouble, especially if Mukhisa is the candidate," said Karani.

Detached from the people

Mukhisa was elected the MP for the larger Kimilili constituency in 1992 and was re-elected in 1997 and 2002.

He lost the seat to Eseli Simiyu in 2007 in a hotly contested election. Eseli is the current MP for Tongaren after the larger Kimilili constituency was split in 2012.

From 2008 to 2012, Mukhisa spent most of his time offering consultancy services outside the country.

He was a member of a team of experts advising the presidents of the nations of the East African Community on how to establish more effective regional economic links.

From 2011 to 2012, he was a consultant for the African Union Commission, where he helped to develop the structure for a pan-African free trade area.

He came back at the end of 2012 and faced off with Wetang'ula for Bungoma senatorial race but was floored.

Karani attributed Mukhisa's unsuccessful bid to his absence in the political scene after losing the parliamentary seat in 2007.

"Mukhisa was and I believe is still one of the finest speakers we have in this region, give him a platform and he will outshine you. His only weakness is that he has been detached from the people for too long. "He is returning to the political scene after losing the last two elections he participated in, that in 2007 and 2013. Now he is going for presidency, what does that tell you? He is locally starting from a negative and nationally moving into unchartered waters," argued Karani.

Political analyst Javan Bigambo opined the former UNCTAD boss lacks the zeal, passion and energy to run a presidential campaign.

He suggested a gubernatorial or senatorial bid would have been an equal match to his low-energy presidential campaign launch.

"At the risk of appearing banefully skeptical of his ambition, cognisant of his low-energy press conference today, I opine that Dr. Mukhisa Kituyi should serve as a Governor or Senator for now, then later, raise his sights to contest the presidency," said Bigambo via his Twitter handle.

During an interview with KTN News hours after announcing his presidential bid, the former Kimilili lawmaker acknowledged the challenge of being away from the people for long but promised to work on a plan to change the perception.

"I have not had an opportunity to talk to the people directly because as the secretary general of UNCTAD you only deal with governments and not people. Before going to UN in September 2013 I was working with the Africa Union," he acknowleged.

Elitist tag

The former UNCTAD boss is also seen as an elitist who hardly interacts with local people in the villages.

Relating well with people from humble backgrounds and having a great sense of humour are crucial tools for political success in Kenya.

Recently, Lurambi MP Titus Khamala asked his constituents not to vote for politicians who do not attend burials.

He specifically mentioned Mukhisa as one of the leaders who do not attend funerals, adding such politicians should be 'fined' by mourners whenever they show up.

A section of his former constituents who spoke to TUKO.co.ke shared similar concerns about Mukhisa's social life.

"He is not accessible and most of us even fear approaching him because we feel he will not listen to us. He is not the kind of a leader you will go to with personal issues like we are used to," said Zack Wechuli, a busnessman from Kumukuywa.

Some accused him of dismal performance as an MP and discredited his presidential bid.

"The reason why we did not re-elect him in 2007 is because we lost confidence in his leadership as a politician. I think Mukhisa is only effective in boardroom leadership," said Geoffery Muyundo, a teacher from Kimilili.

To some, the articulate economist is Kenya's only living hope and should be given a chance to clear the mess.

"I am personally impressed with his record at UNCTAD, I thought he would bring us shame but he did well. He is the man we can trust to lead our country," said Gladys Siboe.

Mukhisa has dismissed the narrative that he is an elitist and has challenged his critics to sit back and watch the space.

Starting today you will see me moving around and talking to the people to change this narrative that has been out there that I am an elitist," said the scholar.

Luhya elders standpoint

A host of Luhya elders threw their weight behind Mudavadi lask week and asked him to go and form alliances with like-minded politicians in the country to seek power in 2022.

The over 500 old men converged at Mudavadi's rural Mululu home in Vihiga county on Saturday, February 20, and gave the ANC leader the blessings to represent the community in national leadership.

This, although not ultimately, will cripple Mukhisa's manoeuvres in Western Kenya and make it difficult for him to marshal enough support to boost his chances of becoming Kenya's fifth president.
