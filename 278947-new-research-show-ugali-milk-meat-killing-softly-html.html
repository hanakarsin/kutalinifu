New research show why ugali, milk and meat could be killing you softly

Posted by Hettie Henneman on Sunday, June 2, 2024

- The research by International Livestock Research Institute (ILRI) was conducted in five counties

- It showed consumption of large amounts of milk, meat and cereals with aflatoxin was harmful

- Milk from farmers and pasteurised one was found to contain aflatoxin levels exceeding FAO limit

- Cereal products contained high levels of aflatoxin exposure due to prolonged humidity in the country and poor grain storage

Kenyans could be exposing themselves to detrimental health risks by consuming large amounts of milk, meat and grain that have aflatoxin levels exceeding internationally accepted limits, a new study shows.

The daunting research findings come even as Kenyans are trying to come to terms with the fact that they could be consuming sugar laced with lethal chemicals including mercury, copper and lead.

The study released in June 2018 by the International Livestock Research Institute (ILRI) and seen by TUKO.co.ke on Saturday, July 7, established that Kenya has aflatoxin levels ranging between 75% to 100%.

The study further showed that 72% of the milk from dairy farmers, 84% from large and medium-scale farmers and 99% of the pasteurised marketed milk had aflatoxin levels exeeding Food and Agricultural Organisation (FAO) limit.

According to the survey, livestock feeds bought from retailers exceeded the limit of the toxic substances hence making consumption of large amounts of meat harmful to humans.

The survey titled Measuring and Mitigating Risk of Mycotoxins in Maize and Dairy Products for Poor Consumers in Kenya was done from samples of livestock feeds collected from farmers in five counties.

Milk samples from those counties had high aflatoxin levels with at least 55% of samples exceeding the standard level.

Consumers of cereal products are not safe either as they risk aflatoxin exposure following prolonged humidity in the country and poor grain storage among smallholder farmers.

“Households are consuming unprecedented levels of aflatoxin, a carcinogenic substance, hence putting their lives in danger while hampering efforts to reduce the country’s growing cancer burden,” said lead researcher Johana Lindahl.

Acute aflatoxicosis, apart from being the leading cause of liver cancer, can lead to depression, anorexia, weight loss, gastrointestinal bleeding, pulmonary edema and stunted growth especially in children.

“Kenya is a hotspot for aflatoxin contamination, especially in maize, and farmers, traders and the general public need to be educated on the dangers and simple prevention measures,’’ said Lindahl.

The survey comes even as there is heightened tension in the country over the safety of sugar sold in local shops following reports that it could be laced with toxic substances.

On Friday, June 22, Kenyans were left perplexed after Interior Cabinet Secretary Fred Matiang'i and Trade Cabinet Secretary Aden Mohammed admittd that seized contraband sugar was contaminated.
