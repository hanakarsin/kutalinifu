US lists Kenya among global abduction hotspots

Posted by Chauncey Koziol on Wednesday, July 10, 2024

Kenya's tourism sector could suffer a major setback following a decision by the United States of America (USA) to list the country as one of the global kidnapping hotspots.

The new travel advisory came with introduction of kidnapping risk indicator to the safety and security messaging for US travellers which may discourage American citizens from visiting the country for tourism.

In a statement issued on Tuesday, April 9, the Department of State indicated it had introduced a new risk indicator to the public travel advisories in order to communicate more clearly to US citizens the risks of kidnapping and hostage taking by criminals and terrorist actors around the world.

"The new 'K' risk indicator is part of our ongoing commitment to provide clear and comprehensive travel safety information to US citizens so they can make informed travel decision," the Department of State noted.

TUKO.co.ke understands the Department issues travel advisories for every country around the world offering standardised levels of advice based on established risk indicators such as natural disasters, crime, terrorism, civil unrest, health and other potential risks.

"The travel advisories for 35 countries have been updated to include a 'K' indicator for the risk of kidnapping and/or hostage taking," the the Department of State announced.

Kenya, Uganda and South Sudan were listed among the 35 countries now considered hotspots for abductions.

The other countries include Afghanistan, Algeria, Angola, Bangladesh, Burkina Faso, Cameroon Central African Republic, Colombia, Democratic Republic of Congo, Ethiopia, Haiti, Iran, Iraq, Lebanon, Malaysia, Mmali, Mexico, Niger, Nigeria and Somalia.

Pakistan, Papua New Guinea, Philipines, Russian Federation, Syria, Sudan, Trinidad, Tobago, Turkey, Ukraine (in Russian-controlled eastern Ukraine), Yemen and Venezuela also made it to the dreaded list.

The US travel advisory update comes days after an American tourist was kidnapped in Uganda together with her tour guide and held hostage for days.

Kimberly Endicott and Jean-Paul Mirenge were abducted by unknown criminals while on an evening game drive in the Queen Elizabeth National Park and later released after a ransom was paid.
