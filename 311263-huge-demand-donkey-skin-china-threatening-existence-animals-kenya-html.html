Huge demand for donkey skin from China threatening existence of the animals in Kenya

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Wednesday, April 17, 2024

Millions of donkeys are at risk because of rapidly growing demand for ‘ejiao’, a product made from donkey skins used in traditional Chinese medicine.

A recent report by the Kenya Agricultural and Livestock Research Organisation (KALRO) predicted that donkeys in Kenya could be effectively wiped out by 2023.

According to a separate report by The Brook Organisation, Donkeys across the globe are being killed for their skins to meet the ever-increasing demand from China.

Ejiao or donkey hide glue, is a gelatinous substance made from boiling the hides of donkeys, which is used in traditional Chinese medicines and beauty products.

Its manufacturers claim the substance has many health benefits, including reducing wrinkles, curing anaemia, boosting energy, enhancing libido, and shrinking tumours.

But there is little scientific evidence to support these claims.

The recent growth in the Chinese middle class has led to a surge in consumer demand for ejiao.

The report by KALRO warned that the welfare of the donkeys at the slaughterhouses is likely to be horrifically compromised, with the number of donkeys being killed set to go up.

The treatment of donkeys in this process is said to be often horrific and has a huge impact on the livelihoods of people, as well as the population of the beasts of burden.

Brooke is now calling for a ban on the trade of donkey skins in Kenya and a crackdown on cross border smuggling of the animals in the country for their skins.

Brooke also wants to see a ban on donkey skin products.

At least 10 African countries have now announced bans or restrictions on the trade, including Mali, Botswana, Tanzania and Niger.

Killing of donkeys in Kenya is legal, with four licensed slaughterhouses, the latest located near the border with Ethiopia which promotes smuggling of donkeys into Kenya.

The report by Brook also showed up to 1,000 donkeys can be legally killed every day, but hundreds more are killed illegally.

Kenya and China on Tuesday, July 23, partnered up in the trade that initially received heavy criticism and according to the PS for trade Chris Kiptoo, the skins have a market in China.

The protocol was signed between the PS, Hamadi Boga of crops, Harry Kimutai of Livestock and development and the Vice Minister, General Administration Customs Zhang Jiwen.

The donkey skin trade is decimating the population of donkeys in some of the world’s most vulnerable communities and Kenya is said to the epicentre of this issue within Africa.
