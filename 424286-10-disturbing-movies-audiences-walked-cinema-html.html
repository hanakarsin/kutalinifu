10 disturbing movies that audiences walked out of in the cinema

Posted by Enda Jantzen on Tuesday, July 23, 2024

The horror genre exploits cultural fears by pushing the boundaries of social norms and taboos. It is not rare to find movies that audiences walked out of due to their extreme profanity and gory imagery. The narrative developed in this subset depict the worst form of human nature, and when the obscenity becomes uncontained, the audience is provoked to walk out of the theatre.

Moviegoers are always thrilled when a new movie is released. But their reservation tends to change upon watching the horror film. Should producers filter scenes that audiences find too obscene or unwatchable? Perhaps some of these films act as mirrors of the sad realities of life that never gets the media’s attention.

Disturbing movies that audiences walked out of in the cinema

The social problems and the dark horrors they illuminate are no longer swept under the carpet. They are being projected on screens. Think of the fear of political revolutions, natural disasters, family rivalry, war, violence and financial security. How far should filmmakers go in portraying these fears in their most extreme forms?

The US, Italy, France and Japan produce the highest number of horror movies that are so disturbing. As liberal societies, these countries often tell tales of their distant past through cinema. The flourishing of horror films in Italy took centre stage after the fall of fascism. So was the extreme profanity woven into Japanese movies after the end of World War II.

Holocaust left indelible echoes of the untold nightmares of totalitarianism. These are terrifying realities that are regarded as too grotesque for public consumption. Some horror films have found a place in the public domain and are accessible in local cinemas. However, you can only watch these shocking movies at your own risk:

2. Nekromantik (1987)

IMDB rating: 5/10 Runtime: 1h 15m

The title of this cult-horror film suggests it has content that you might find repulsive and utterly disgusting. True to its name, Nekromantik traces a couple who redefine the contextual boundaries of sexual relations with dead bodies. The film was banned in several countries including, Australia, Denmark and Finland, due to graphic necrophilia content.

The plot follows Rob, a street sweeper who sneaks a corpse from a scene of an accident to his home. The body was not just a souvenir but an object that stirred weird desires and deviant motives. Betty, the wife, joins him in necrophilic acts that are too unsavoury even for horror fans.

6. A Serbian Film (2011)

This shocking film is based on a porn star going through financial hardships before landing a film job. It was late when he discovered the role was to perform paedophilia and necrophilia themed scenes in a snuff film. After it debuted, many movie fans were dismayed with the explicit way taboo was exhibited by the movie director, Spasojević.

10. Cannibal Holocaust (1980)

IMDb rating: 5.8/10 Runtime: 1h 38m

Claimed as one of the most controversial movies in history by Entertainment Weekly, Cannibal Holocaust is a tale of cannibalism, and sexual assaults staged at an Amazon forest. It was banned in over 50 countries after its release in 1980. The director, Ruggero Deodato found himself on the receiving end upon being arrested and pressed to attest acts depicted in the film were special effects.

Cannibal Holocaust traces the heart-wrenching experiences of anthropologists on a journey to the Amazon jungle to trace lost colleagues. They uncovered a trail of cannibalistic acts perpetrated by the natives. The natives later descended on them, killing them in a bizarre turn of events.
